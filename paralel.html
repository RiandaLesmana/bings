Parallel Structure Lesson

Introduction

Parallel structure, also known as parallelism, refers to using the same grammatical form or structure in a series of words, phrases, or clauses. Maintaining parallelism enhances clarity, balance, and readability in sentences.

Error Recognition

One common error with parallel structure is when the elements in a series do not match in terms of form or structure. Let's look at an example:

Correct: I like to swim, hike, and read books.

Wrong: I like to swim, hiking, and to read books.

Explanation

Parallel structure should be maintained when listing or comparing items in a series. Here are some key guidelines:

Parallel Verb Forms

Use the same verb form for items in a series.

Correct: She enjoys hiking, swimming, and running.

Wrong: She enjoys hiking, swims, and running.

Parallel Gerund Phrases

If using gerund phrases, ensure they have the same structure.

Correct: He likes reading books, listening to music, and playing sports.

Wrong: He likes reading books, listens to music, and playing sports.

Parallel Infinitive Phrases

If using infinitive phrases, maintain parallelism.

Correct: The students were asked to study hard, to complete assignments, and to participate actively.

Wrong: The students were asked to study hard, complete assignments, and actively participating.

Parallel Clauses

When using clauses, ensure they have parallel structure.

Correct: He is either going to the movies or staying at home.

Wrong: He is either going to the movies or he will stay at home.

Summary

Parallel structure improves the flow and clarity of sentences. Remember to use consistent grammatical forms when listing or comparing items in a series.
